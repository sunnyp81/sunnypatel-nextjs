# Sunny Patel — Brand Voice Guide

> This document defines the voice for all content published under Sunny Patel's name — sunnypatel.co.uk, LinkedIn, email outreach, client reports, and any site where Sunny is the author. It applies to both audiences: SEO professionals and business founders/investors.

---

## The Voice in One Sentence

**A practitioner who shows the work, not a consultant who sells the idea of work.**

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Data First, Opinion Second

Every claim is backed by a number, a screenshot, or a Search Console export. The voice earns authority through evidence, not assertion. If there's no data yet, say so — honesty about incomplete results is more credible than inflated conclusions.

**Do:** "Average position improved from 31.6 to 25.6 within 10 days."
**Don't:** "We saw significant improvements in search visibility across the board."

### 2. Direct, Not Aggressive

Sentences are short. Points are clear. There is no hedging with qualifiers ("perhaps", "it could be argued that", "in many cases"). But directness never becomes confrontational or dismissive of the reader. State what is true. Move on.

**Do:** "Most site owners think more pages means more chances to rank. The opposite is usually true."
**Don't:** "It's a common misconception that many site owners unfortunately fall into, where they believe..."

### 3. Practitioner, Not Professor

Sunny writes as someone who does the work daily across 44 real websites — not as someone who studies SEO from the outside. The perspective is always from inside the console, inside the codebase, inside the data. Theory is only referenced when it explains what happened in practice.

**Do:** "I found this same issue on 23 of 44 sites."
**Don't:** "Research suggests this is a widespread industry concern affecting numerous websites."

### 4. Honest About Limitations

When results are early, say they're early. When a fix didn't work, say it didn't work. When the data is inconclusive, say that. This is the single biggest differentiator from agency content, which only publishes wins.

**Do:** "Some of these fixes are too recent for definitive results. I'll update this post when the data lands."
**Don't:** Omit the caveat entirely and present partial data as proof.

### 5. Respect the Reader's Intelligence

No defining basic terms unprompted. No "In today's digital landscape" throat-clearing. No explaining what Google is. The reader is a business owner or a technical professional. They know what search engines are. Skip to the part that helps them.

**Do:** Jump straight into the finding, the fix, or the insight.
**Don't:** "Search Engine Optimisation, commonly known as SEO, is the process of..."

## Tone Spectrum

The voice sits at a specific point on each of these axes:

| Axis | Position | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Formal ← → Casual | **65% toward casual** | Professional but not corporate. No slang, no "mate", no exclamation marks. Contractions are always used (it's, you're, don't). |
| Technical ← → Accessible | **60% toward technical** | Technical terms are used without apology when they're the right word. But jargon is never used to impress — only to be precise. A founder should understand every sentence; an SEO should find it credible. |
| Confident ← → Humble | **70% toward confident** | Sunny owns his expertise. 15+ years, 44 sites, real results. But confidence is shown through specifics, never through superlatives ("best", "leading", "world-class"). |
| Warm ← → Detached | **55% toward warm** | Not cold or clinical. There is a human behind the writing. But warmth comes from honesty and usefulness, not from friendliness or humour. |

## Language Rules

### Always

- **British English.** Optimise, analyse, colour, behaviour, programme (but "program" for code). -ise endings, not -ize.
- **Contractions.** It's, you're, don't, won't, they'll. Never "it is" or "you are" — that reads stiff.
- **Active voice.** "I fixed 872 links" not "872 links were fixed."
- **First person singular.** "I" not "we". Sunny is one person, not a team pretending to be one.
- **Specific numbers.** "23 of 44 sites" not "over half". "Position 31.6 to 25.6" not "a significant improvement."
- **Present tense for ongoing truths.** "Google truncates titles at 55-60 characters" not "Google has been known to truncate."

### Never

- **Superlatives without evidence.** No "best", "leading", "top", "world-class", "cutting-edge" unless backed by a specific, cited fact.
- **Filler openings.** No "In today's digital landscape", "As we all know", "It's no secret that", "In the ever-evolving world of SEO."
- **Corporate third person.** No "Sunny Patel delivers results" or "Our team provides." Write as a person, not a brand brochure.
- **Exclamation marks.** Never. Not even one. Urgency comes from the data, not from punctuation.
- **Emojis.** Never in written content. Not in blog posts, not in emails, not in reports.
- **Name-dropping.** Don't reference mentors, trainers, or industry figures to borrow credibility. Sunny's authority comes from his own work.
- **Buzzword clusters.** No "leveraging synergies to drive holistic growth." If a sentence could appear in any consultant's LinkedIn post, rewrite it.
- **"Just" and "simply".** These minimise difficulty and patronise the reader. If something is straightforward, the instructions will make that obvious.

## Vocabulary

### Preferred Terms

| Use | Instead Of |
|-----|-----------|
| fix | remediate, address, action |
| improve | enhance, elevate, uplift |
| check | audit, assess, evaluate (unless it's a formal audit) |
| show | demonstrate, illustrate, showcase |
| find | identify, uncover, discover |
| tell | inform, advise, communicate |
| break / broken | suboptimal, underperforming |
| page | asset, digital property |
| site | web property, online presence |
| money | revenue, monetary value (use the actual number instead) |
| Google Search Console | GSC (after first mention) |
| position | ranking (both acceptable) |

These can be used freely. The audience knows them:

canonical, trailing slash, noindex, sitemap, crawl budget, backlinks, CTR, impressions, SERP, schema markup, Core Web Vitals, indexation, robots.txt, hreflang, 301 redirect, topical authority, keyword cannibalization

These need a brief inline explanation for the founder audience:

programmatic SEO, semantic SEO, entity SEO, AI Overviews, llms.txt, hub-and-spoke architecture

## Structure Patterns

### Blog Posts

- **Opening:** One paragraph maximum. State what the post covers and why it matters. No preamble.
- **Headings:** Direct statements or questions. "Why This Matters" not "An Exploration of the Implications."
- **Data presentation:** Bold the key number. Use tables for comparisons. Use inline SVGs for complex data.
- **Internal links:** Natural, contextual. Never "click here." Always anchor text that describes the destination.
- **Closing:** One sentence CTA maximum. No paragraph-long sales pitches. If the content did its job, the reader knows how to reach you.

### Service Pages

- **Lead with the problem,** not the service name. The reader is searching because something isn't working.
- **Comparison tables** between consultant vs agency, or before vs after. Concrete, not abstract.
- **One testimonial** if available. Client's actual words, not paraphrased.
- **Pricing transparency.** "From £500" is better than "Contact us for a quote."

### Email / Outreach

- **No pitch.** Share something useful. If it's relevant to the recipient, they'll reply.
- **Short.** Under 100 words. No formatting, no bold, no bullet points. It should read like a message from a person, not a campaign.
- **Sign off as "Sunny"** — not "Sunny Patel, SEO Consultant, sunnypatel.co.uk" with a full signature block.

### Client Reports

- **Lead with what changed and why it matters to the business.** Not a list of tasks completed.
- **Numbers with context.** "Impressions up 134%, driven by the title template fix deployed on Apr 2" — not just a graph.
- **Honest about what didn't work** as well as what did.

## Audience Adaptation

The voice stays the same for both audiences. The depth changes.

### For SEO Professionals / Peers

- Technical terms without explanation.
- Code examples, config snippets, GSC screenshots.
- Methodology discussion (why this approach over alternatives).
- Internal links to technical service pages.

### For Founders / Investors / Business Owners

- Same directness, same data-first approach.
- Technical terms used but followed by what it means for their business.
- Frame everything in revenue impact or competitive advantage.
- "This is costing you leads" matters more than "this is causing duplicate canonicals."
- Internal links to blog posts that explain concepts.

## The Anti-Voice

If you're unsure whether something fits, check it against these. If it sounds like any of these, rewrite it:

- **Generic SEO agency website.** "We drive results for businesses of all sizes through our proprietary methodology."
- **LinkedIn thought leader.** "Here are my 7 learnings from scaling a portfolio of websites. Thread."
- **Academic paper.** "This analysis demonstrates a statistically significant correlation between canonical URL consolidation and improved SERP positioning."
- **Hustler/guru.** "Want to know the SECRET to ranking 44 sites? Most people won't tell you this."
- **Robotic AI content.** "In the realm of search engine optimisation, it is widely acknowledged that technical factors play a crucial role."

## Examples — Before and After

**Before (generic agency):**
> At Sunny Patel SEO, we believe in delivering comprehensive, data-driven SEO solutions tailored to each client's unique needs. Our holistic approach ensures sustainable growth across all digital channels.

**After (Sunny's voice):**
> I run 44 websites across different niches. The same three technical issues appeared on nearly every one. Here's what I found and what I did about it.

---

**Before (over-hedged):**
> It's possible that in some cases, title tag truncation may potentially be contributing to lower-than-expected click-through rates for certain pages.

**After (Sunny's voice):**
> 1,546 pages were ranking on page one of Google. The CTR was 0.21%. The titles were being truncated because they pulled from a government database that wasn't designed for search results.

---

**Before (salesy):**
> Don't let your competitors steal your traffic. Book a free consultation today and discover how our proven SEO strategies can transform your online presence.

**After (Sunny's voice):**
> If you'd like me to run a similar analysis across your portfolio, get in touch. I do this every day — not as a one-off audit, but as ongoing technical management across dozens of sites.

## Credibility Signals

These are the facts that establish authority. Use them where relevant, not as a preamble to every piece of content:

- **44 websites managed simultaneously** — real portfolio, not client sites
- **15+ years in SEO** — since before Panda, Penguin, or AI Overviews existed
- **200,000+ pages across the portfolio** — programmatic SEO at scale
- **Real GSC data published** — not anonymised agency case studies
- **Multiple tech stacks** — Astro, Next.js, WordPress, static HTML
- **Reading / Berkshire based** — local credibility for UK clients

Use one or two per piece. Never list them all. The work demonstrates the rest.
